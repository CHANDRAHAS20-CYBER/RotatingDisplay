**Rotating Photo Display**

This is a romantic automated photo display built around an ESP32-C3 SuperMini microcontroller. The device is designed to rotate physically while cycling through 15 photos on a screen, playing background music, and showing a personalized welcome message on startup. It requires no user interaction after power-on — everything runs automatically.

The main controller is the ESP32-C3 SuperMini, a compact Wi-Fi and Bluetooth capable microcontroller running at 3.3V logic. It handles all tasks simultaneously — driving the display over SPI, controlling the servo motor with PWM signals, and communicating with the audio module over UART.

The display is a 2.8 inch ILI9341 TFT screen with a resolution of 240 by 320 pixels, connected to the ESP32-C3 via SPI. It uses GPIO 4 for the clock line, GPIO 6 for MOSI, GPIO 5 for MISO, GPIO 7 for chip select, GPIO 8 for data/command, and GPIO 10 for reset. The display operates in landscape mode during photo playback, giving a 320 by 240 pixel viewing area. A MADCTL register value of 0xE0 is applied to correct the BGR color channel order that is common in clone ILI9341 panels. Photos are stored as RGB565 arrays in flash memory using PROGMEM, with each image being 240 by 320 pixels in portrait orientation matching the welcome screen layout. Images are rendered row by row using a line buffer to reduce RAM usage. Transitions between photos use a 16-step crossfade blending algorithm that mixes pixel colors from the current and next image.

The audio module is a DFPlayer Mini, which is a self-contained MP3 player that reads audio files from a micro SD card and drives a speaker directly. It connects to the ESP32-C3 via UART using GPIO 20 as RX and GPIO 21 as TX, communicating at 9600 baud. A 1 kilo-ohm resistor must be placed on the RX line of the DFPlayer to prevent communication errors. The module supports up to 30 volume levels and the firmware sets it to level 22 by default. It stores up to 15 songs on the SD card, which play sequentially and automatically advance to the next track when the current one finishes.

The motor is a continuous-rotation servo connected to GPIO 9. Unlike a standard positional servo, a continuous-rotation servo spins indefinitely in either direction based on the PWM pulse width sent to it. A pulse of 1500 microseconds stops the motor, 1350 microseconds drives it clockwise, and 1650 microseconds drives it counter-clockwise. The servo is powered from a dedicated supply rail with parallel decoupling capacitors to prevent voltage spikes from interfering with the ESP32 and display. On power-on, the servo receives a 1500 microsecond stop pulse for one second to settle at a neutral state before the rotation cycle begins.

The rotation behavior follows a fixed pattern. The servo spins clockwise for 9 seconds, during which 3 photos are shown at equal time intervals — one at 0 seconds, one at 3 seconds, and one at 6 seconds into the sweep. After the 9 second sweep, the servo stops for 3.5 seconds. It then spins counter-clockwise for another 9 seconds with 3 more photos advancing on the same timing. After another 3.5 second pause, the cycle repeats. This means every full back-and-forth rotation displays 6 photos. All 15 photos cycle continuously and loop back to the first after the last one.

On startup, before switching to photo mode, the display shows a welcome screen in portrait orientation. The screen has a gradient background going from deep blue at the top to dark green at the bottom, a double border in pink and lavender, decorative sparkle shapes at all four corners, a greeting in gold text, the name Priyanka in white text flanked by large pink hearts, a subtitle message in pink and gold text, a row of alternating sparkles, a strip of hearts in varying sizes, and a small tagline at the bottom. This screen displays for 4 seconds and then clears before the photo slideshow begins.

The power setup requires two rails. The ESP32-C3 and display run on a clean 3.3V regulated supply. The servo should have its own supply, either 5V or as per the servo's rated voltage, with the ground connected back to the ESP32-C3 ground to form a common reference. Mixing the servo power with the microcontroller supply without proper decoupling causes resets and display glitches due to the current spikes a motor produces.
